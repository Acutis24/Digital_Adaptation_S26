In 2019, the Pew Research Center published a survey that should have shaken the Catholic Church to its core: only thirty-one percent of self-identified American Catholics believe the bread and wine at Mass actually become the Body and Blood of Christ (Smith). The other sixty-nine percent think it is symbolic. These are not people who have left the faith. They are still filling the pews on Sunday, just without believing the central thing their Church teaches about what is happening at the altar in front of them. Something went badly wrong, and it did not happen by accident.
The story leads back to 1969 and the introduction of the Novus Ordo Missae, the new form of the Roman Rite Mass promulgated by Pope Paul VI following the Second Vatican Council. Defenders of the reform point to the Council’s own document on the liturgy, Sacrosanctum Concilium, as the authorization for what followed. And they are right that the Council called for reform. What they cannot so easily defend is the gap between what the Council asked for and what was actually delivered. While the Second Vatican Council’s vision for liturgical renewal was measured and theologically careful, Paul VI’s implementation of it through the Novus Ordo overshot that mandate, driven largely by one man, Archbishop Annibale Bugnini, whose reform strategy was more ambitious than the Council fathers ever approved. The consequence was not a renewed Church but a confused one, and ordinary Catholics paid the price in lost belief.
[image: tlm-mass.jpg | The Traditional Latin Mass, the form of the Roman Rite the Novus Ordo replaced.]
It matters to start with what the Council actually authorized, because Bugnini’s defenders consistently invoke it as cover for the full scope of the reform. Sacrosanctum Concilium called for greater active participation by the faithful, permitted vernacular languages in parts of the Mass, and asked that anything not genuinely useful be removed (Sacrosanctum Concilium 14). The document’s tone is conservative. It speaks of preserving what is good, of organic development, of change only where genuinely and certainly needed. The Council was not asking for a new rite. It was asking for a carefully reformed version of the old one. What the bishops voted for in 1963 and what Catholics began receiving in 1969 were not the same thing.
The man responsible for that gap was Annibale Bugnini. As secretary of the Consilium, the body charged with implementing the Council’s liturgical reforms, Bugnini held more influence over the shape of the new Mass than any other single figure. French historian Yves Chiron, whose biography of Bugnini is the most thorough scholarly treatment of his role, documents how Bugnini enjoyed unprecedented access to Paul VI and used it to drive the reform well beyond what Sacrosanctum Concilium had mandated (Chiron 2). The controversy, as Chiron frames it through Alcuin Reid’s foreword, is not that reform happened, but that Bugnini orchestrated a substantial rupture with the Church’s liturgical tradition rather than the organic development the Council had called for (Chiron 1–2).
[image: bugnini.jpg | Archbishop Annibale Bugnini, Secretary of the Consilium charged with implementing the Council’s liturgical reforms.]
Bugnini’s own words confirm the strategy. In a 1961 address to the Preparatory Commission, he instructed his colleagues to write proposals carefully so that "much is said without seeming to say anything," leaving doors open for postconciliar changes that could not yet be explicitly requested (Chiron 4). This was not a man faithfully executing a mandate. This was a man using the Council as a vehicle for a broader program of reform that the bishops were never asked to approve. Paul VI, who had the authority to impose limits and did not, bears his share of the responsibility. He signed the Novus Ordo, gave Bugnini the access he needed, and continued working with him even as resistance grew inside the Church.
That resistance was serious. In September 1969, Cardinals Alfredo Ottaviani and Antonio Bacci delivered a formal theological study to Paul VI, warning that the Novus Ordo represented a departure from the Catholic theology of the Mass as defined at the Council of Trent. Ottaviani was not a peripheral figure. He had run the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, the Vatican’s own guardian of Catholic teaching. When he concluded that the new Mass had doctrinal problems, the objection carried real weight. Paul VI referred the study for review, the review dismissed most of it, and the reform proceeded. The warnings were acknowledged just enough to say they had been considered, then set aside.
[image: paul-vi.jpg | Pope Paul VI, who promulgated the Novus Ordo Missae in 1969 and gave Bugnini the authority to execute the reform.]
To understand what was at stake in all of this, it is necessary to be clear about what the Church actually teaches about the Eucharist. The doctrine is more precise than most people realize. At the moment of consecration, the substance of the bread and wine is transformed into the Body and Blood of Christ, while the outward appearances remain unchanged. This is transubstantiation (Catechism of the Catholic Church, par. 1376), and it is not a minor point of theology. The Catechism identifies the Eucharist as "the source and summit of the Christian life" (par. 1324). Everything else in Catholic worship points toward this moment. Central to making it happen is the ordained priest, who acts not as a community facilitator but in persona Christi, in the person of Christ, at the altar (Presbyterorum Ordinis 5). That distinction, between the priest as a unique mediator and the priest as a communal representative, turns out to be one of the things the post-conciliar period most consistently blurred.
Three interconnected failures explain why belief in the Real Presence collapsed so broadly after 1969. The first is catechetical. When the Pew data surfaced, bishops and Catholic educators responded with alarm, calling for a serious renewal of Eucharistic catechesis and acknowledging that religious education had grown thin and inconsistent in the decades since Vatican II (McKeown and Jones). The Novus Ordo was designed to be more comprehensible than the old Latin rite, but comprehensible and explained are not the same thing. The ceremonial gravity of the Tridentine Mass had done quiet doctrinal work. It communicated, through its formality and its reverence, that something extraordinary was happening at the altar. When that gravity was softened and the explanations did not follow, people filled the gap with the most intuitive interpretation available: it is a symbolic meal. The reform created the conditions for that misreading, and the Church’s catechetical infrastructure was not strong enough to correct it.
The second failure was structural. The flexibility Bugnini built into the Novus Ordo produced a liturgy that looked and felt radically different from one parish to the next. Lucas Van Rompay, Sam Miglarese, and David Morgan, writing in The Long Shadow of Vatican II, document how ordinary Catholics in the post-conciliar period experienced the Church’s reforms as perpetually unfinished, with the meaning of the Council’s changes remaining genuinely contested at the local level (Van Rompay et al. 7). A doctrine as precise and demanding as transubstantiation requires a stable liturgical context to take root. When the Mass changes depending on the parish, the priest, and the decade, the doctrinal content it is meant to carry loses its footing. That instability was not an accident of implementation. It was engineered into the reform from the start.
[image: novus-ordo-mass.jpg | A Novus Ordo Mass. The flexibility built into the new rite produced a liturgy that looked and felt different from one parish to the next.]
The third failure was theological. Vatican II’s Lumen Gentium recovered the important idea that the whole Church shares in Christ’s priesthood, describing the faithful as a priestly people (Lumen Gentium 9–10). In the hands of careful theologians, this is a legitimate and valuable insight. Filtered through the post-conciliar parish, it often became something else: a flattening of the distinction between the ordained priest and the rest of the congregation. Matthew Lamb and Matthew Levering argue that many of the post-conciliar problems in Catholic life stem from reading the Council as a rupture rather than a development (Lamb and Levering 4). That misreading was made easier by a reform process that had already been pulling the priesthood’s role toward the communal and away from the sacrificial. When the priest no longer seems categorically different from the people he serves, the theological ground for believing that his words transform bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ begins to erode.
Defenders of the reform will raise two objections worth taking seriously. The first is that Bugnini was not acting alone and that Paul VI reviewed and approved everything, which makes the reform legitimately authoritative regardless of Bugnini’s personal ambitions. This is true as far as it goes, but it sidesteps the question of whether Paul VI was given complete and accurate information about how far the reform was departing from the Council’s actual text. The Ottaviani Intervention suggests that serious concerns existed at the highest levels of the Church. The second objection is empirical: a 2024 study reported by EWTN Great Britain found that globally, as many as two-thirds of Catholics still affirm belief in the Real Presence, which complicates the picture of total collapse ("New Study"). This is a fair point. The erosion is not universal, and the American data may reflect conditions specific to the United States. But two-thirds believing is still one-third not believing, and in a Church whose entire sacramental life is ordered around the Eucharist, that is not a number to be satisfied with.
The Second Vatican Council asked for a careful renewal of the Mass. What it got was something more radical, shaped by one man’s long-term strategy and enabled by a pope who gave him the authority to execute it. Bugnini’s own words reveal a reformer who knew he was working beyond his mandate and was careful to leave that fact obscured. The Cardinals who raised the alarm were heard just enough to be dismissed. The catechesis that should have accompanied the new rite never materialized at scale. The flexibility designed into the Novus Ordo produced instability. And the theological recovery of lay dignity slid, in practice, into a confusion about the priesthood that made the Real Presence harder to believe. None of this was inevitable. These were choices, made by people with names and titles, and the ordinary Catholics sitting in the pews absorbed the consequences. The Church’s ongoing Eucharistic Revival is an acknowledgment, however indirect, that something went wrong. The Catechism calls the Eucharist the source and summit of Catholic life. Recovering it as a living belief is not a restoration project. It is the whole point.
